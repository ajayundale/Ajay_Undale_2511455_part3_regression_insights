# Regression-Based Business Insights & Model Interpretation

## Project Overview

This project applies linear regression analysis to identify the business factors most strongly associated with monthly sales across a retail store network. The analysis was performed using Microsoft Excel and focuses on supporting business decision-making through statistical modelling and interpretation.

The objective is to help retail leadership understand how operational and business variables influence monthly sales, enabling data-driven decisions related to marketing, inventory management, staffing, and store operations.

---

# Business Problem

The retail leadership team wants to understand the key drivers of monthly sales performance across stores. Specifically, the business aims to answer the following questions:

* Which business factors have the strongest association with monthly sales?
* Which variables should leadership prioritise to improve sales performance?
* Are certain store formats associated with higher sales?
* Which operational variables have limited statistical influence?
* How reliable are the regression models for business decision-making?

Regression analysis was used to quantify these relationships and provide evidence-based recommendations.

---

# Dataset Description

The dataset contains monthly operational and sales information for multiple retail stores.

The dataset includes both numerical and categorical variables representing marketing, customer activity, store operations, and financial performance.

### Data cleaning

- There were 8 records where `customer_rating` was blank. Filled it with the average of `customer_rating` from provided data.
- There were 6 records where `competitor_distance_km` was blank. Filled it with the average of `competitor_distance_km` from provided data.

# Dummy Variable Approach

The categorical variable **store_type** was converted into dummy variables before multiple regression analysis.

The following categories were identified:

* Airport
* High Street
* Mall
* Residential

To avoid the Dummy Variable Trap, **Airport** was selected as the **reference category**.

The following dummy variables were created:

* `Dummy_HighStreet`	
* `Dummy_Mall`	
* `Dummy_Residential`

Interpretation:

* A coefficient for `Dummy_HighStreet` represents the expected difference in monthly sales between High Street stores and Airport stores, holding all other variables constant.
* A coefficient for `Dummy_Mall` represents the expected difference in monthly sales between Mall stores and Airport stores.

# Regression Approach

Three regression models were developed.

## Model 1 – Simple Linear Regression

Dependent Variable:

* Monthly Sales

Independent Variable:

* Marketing Spend

Purpose:

Evaluate whether marketing expenditure is associated with higher monthly sales.

---

## Model 2 – Simple Linear Regression

Dependent Variable:

* Monthly Sales

Independent Variable:

* Footfall

Purpose:

Measure the relationship between customer traffic and monthly sales.

---

## Model 3 – Multiple Linear Regression

Dependent Variable:

* Monthly Sales

Independent Variables:

* Dummy_HighStreet
* Dummy_Mall
* Dummy_Residential
* marketing_spend
* footfall
* avg_discount_pct
* staff_count
* inventory_availability_pct
* competitor_distance_km
* holiday_flag
* customer_rating

Purpose:

Estimate the combined influence of multiple business variables on monthly sales while controlling for the effect of other predictors.

---

# Model Comparison Summary

Three regression models were compared using the following evaluation criteria:

* R-squared
* Adjusted R-squared
* Regression coefficients
* Statistical significance (P-values)
* Business interpretation
* Overall usefulness for decision-making

The multiple regression model demonstrated the highest explanatory power because it incorporated multiple operational and business variables simultaneously.

---

# Final Model Selected

The multiple regression model was selected as the preferred analytical model because it:

* Uses several important business drivers simultaneously.
* Controls for interactions among predictors.
* Provides stronger explanatory power than individual simple regression models.
* Produces more comprehensive insights for business decision-making.

The final regression equation and coefficient interpretation are documented in:

* `outputs/model_equations.md`

---

# Business Recommendation

Based on the regression analysis, leadership should focus on the variables that demonstrate statistically significant relationships with monthly sales.

Potential priority areas include:

* Increasing marketing effectiveness
* Improving inventory availability
* Driving customer footfall
* Evaluating differences in store formats

Variables with high P-values should be interpreted cautiously because they may not have a statistically meaningful relationship with monthly sales within the current model.

Regression results should be used together with operational knowledge when making strategic business decisions.

---

# Assumptions and Limitations

This analysis has several limitations.

* Regression measures association rather than causation.
* External influences such as economic conditions, competitor promotions, weather, and local events are not included in the dataset.
* Results depend on the quality and completeness of the available data.
* Relationships identified in this analysis may change over time as business conditions evolve.

Consequently, regression findings should support managerial decision-making rather than replace business judgement.
